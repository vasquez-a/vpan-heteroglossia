heteroglossia

they say you don't get a choice in your native language
but this isn't true
if your house is bilingual
if languages are all around you
then you do have a choice
and your choice is english.
so you let things
like where is
el techo
and what time is
ahorita
and the difference between
servilleta and cepillo
fall to the wayside.
but some of it sticks
despite years of rust
other things
show up in ways you wouldn't expect.
they say the goal of world language education is real-life communication
so when the time comes for you to take foreign language classes
they give you a choice on what language you want
but you don't really have a choice
and class does not teach
your classmates the quirks of your speech
and class will not teach
you the quirks of your family's speech
you need the structure of a class to feel your progress
but you won't remember
what are
all of the greetings
or
how to conjugate every verb in the past perfect tense
or
passages of Don Quixote
all of the things you'll learn at the desk
what you'll find most important
is what you'll learn
by listening to conversations
in the car
or around the kitchen table
or on the back porch
when you can barely follow what's happening
but you try to follow nonetheless
and once you find
the little things that mark your progress
you will know
why you keep trying to learn it.
